Programme Overview

The Gaudiya Studies Programme is designed to help you learn through self-study and through dialogue with a scholar who is an expert on the topic. Each week, you will meet with a tutor to discuss a particular aspect of the Gauḍīya tradition, after you have invested time into reading and exploring the topic by yourself.

The conversation will be informed and you will come to each session not just with questions, but also with information and ideas that allow you to get much more out of each session.

Previous students have recommended setting aside a total of 10–12 hours per week, broken down as follows:

  • 6–8 hours — Reading and reflecting on the week's topic
  • 2 hours — Cohort Discussion Time
  • 2 hours — Weekly Tutorial with a Scholar

Tutorials — The weekly interactive learning and teaching space with a scholar.

Community Time — The cohort meets between tutorials to review their readings, explore topics together, and discuss ideas. A minimum of 2 hours per week is recommended, with days and timings set by the cohort. No scholars attend these conversations.

Readings — Each scholar has provided a list of topical and relevant readings, found within their respective folders on the Google Drive.

Presentation — Students desiring certification should make a 5–7 minute presentation reflecting on any topic within the programme or its impact on them, followed by 5 minutes for questions from the cohort.

Zoom — All tutorials, cohort discussions, and online meetings use a single dedicated link. Save it somewhere accessible.

Google Drive — Contains all weekly readings (mostly PDF), and a Google Document per session for you to submit pre-tutorial questions. Scholars may review this document before the session to shape their tutorial.

WhatsApp / Email — As a group, create a quick communication channel for updates, changes, and reminders.

Please note: no recordings will be made during tutorials, and students are asked not to record any tutorials.

While all materials are provided digitally, you may wish to acquire physical copies of the following books. The first is used across several sessions; the others are used extensively in individual sessions.

  • Ravi M. Gupta (ed.), Caitanya Vaiṣṇava Philosophy: Tradition, Reason and Devotion. Ashgate/Routledge, 2014. ISBN 9781138248854
  • Ravi M. Gupta & Kenneth R. Valpey (ed.), The Bhāgavata Purāṇa: Sacred Text and Living Tradition. Columbia University Press, 2013. ISBN 9780231169011
  • Joseph O'Connell, Caitanya Vaiṣṇavism in Bengal. Routledge, 2018. ISBN 9780367583538
  • Måns Broo, As Good as God: The Guru in Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism. Åbo Akademi University Press, 2003. ISBN 9517651325

Tip: bookfinder.com is a useful tool for finding cheaper copies online.

Scholars This Term
Gopīnāthācārya (Dr. Rembert Lutjeharms) is the Librarian and a research lecturer at the Oxford Centre for Hindu Studies. He is the author of A Vaiṣṇava Poet in Early Modern Bengal: Kavikarṇapūra's Splendour of Speech (Oxford University Press, 2018), and is an editor of the Journal of Hindu Studies. Together with Dr. Kiyokazu Okita (Sofia University, Tokyo), he leads a research project on the early history of Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism: The Gosvāmī Era: The Founding of Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism in Early Modern South Asia. He is also a member of ISKCON's Śāstric Advisory Committee. His main research interests are Sanskrit devotional poetry and poetics, the history of Vedānta, and the early intellectual history of the Gauḍīya tradition.
Bhṛgupāda (Dr. Måns Broo) is a university researcher in comparative religion at Åbo Akademi University, Finland. His main research interests include yoga — both its history and contemporary forms — and the intersections between Vaiṣṇavism and Tantrism in pre-modern Bengal. He is the author of As Good as God: The Guru in Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism (Åbo Akademi University Press, 2003). He has critically edited and translated the Rādhā Tantra (Routledge, 2017), a publication which won the Donner Institute's Research Prize 2017, and has translated the Upaniṣads into Swedish. He is currently engaged in compiling a critical edition and translation of the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava ritual compilation Hari-bhakti-vilāsa.
Kṛṣṇa Kṣetra Swami received a D.Phil. from the University of Oxford (St. Cross College) for his dissertation on Caitanya Vaiṣṇava image worship, published as Attending Krsna's Image: Caitanya Vaisnava Murti-seva as Devotional Truth (Routledge, 2006). He is a Research Fellow of the Oxford Centre for Hindu Studies and has taught at Bhaktivedanta College, Radhadesh since 2002. He has taught survey courses in Indian religions at the University of Florida and at Chinese University of Hong Kong, and has lectured at several universities in China. He served as ISKCON's GBC minister for Deity worship from 1995 to 2005 and is currently a member of the Śāstric Advisory Committee.
Shaunaka Rishi Das is the Director and founder of the Oxford Centre for Hindu Studies (OCHS), and Hindu Chaplain to the University of Oxford. He was Chairman of ISKCON Communications Europe from 1991–2003, an executive member of ISKCON's Ministry of Educational Development from 1996–2010, and a trustee of Bhaktivedanta College in Belgium. He is also a frequent broadcaster on BBC Radio 4. His interests include education, comparative theology, communication, and leadership.
Rādhikā Ramaṇa (Prof. Ravi M. Gupta) holds the Charles Redd Chair of Religious Studies at Utah State University. He is the author of The Caitanya Vaiṣṇava Vedānta of Jīva Gosvāmī (Routledge, 2007), co-editor of The Bhāgavata Purāṇa: Sacred Text and Living Tradition (Columbia University Press, 2013), editor of Caitanya Vaiṣṇava Philosophy: Tradition Reason and Devotion (Ashgate, 2014), and co-author of The Bhāgavata Purāṇa: Selected Readings (Columbia University Press, 2016). Together with Dr. Kenneth R. Valpey he leads the OCHS's Bhāgavata Purāṇa Research Project. He is also a member of ISKCON's Śāstric Advisory Committee.
Kiyokazu Okita is an assistant professor at the Faculty of Liberal Arts, Sophia University, and a research fellow at the OCHS, as well as visiting faculty at the Center for the Study of Hindu Traditions, University of Florida. He obtained his D.Phil. from the Faculty of Theology and Religion, University of Oxford in 2011. His doctoral thesis focuses on Vaiṣṇava Vedānta in early modern North India, and was published as Hindu Theology in Early Modern South Asia (Oxford University Press, 2014). He was a JSPS postdoctoral research fellow at Kyoto University, a visiting research fellow at the University of Hamburg, and held a competitive research position at the Hakubi Center for Advanced Research, Kyoto University (2013–2017). Together with Dr. Rembert Lutjeharms at OCHS, he leads the collaborative research project The Gosvāmī Era: The Founding of Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism in Early Modern South Asia.
